Case Study: Uber Driver Cancellation Content Redesign

A before and after content transformation project demonstrating modular design, HTML authoring, and AI-assisted workflows

Project goal: Identify a gap in Uber's existing rider support content, redesign it into a modular agent-ready format, and hand-code it in HTML following content architecture and design system standards.

The Problem: What the Original Content Looked Like

Uber's existing cancellation content mixed rider and driver cancellation policies together in a single dense block of text with no clear structure or visual hierarchy. An agent trying to help a rider who was charged after a driver canceled would have to read through the entire article to find the relevant policy — slowing down resolution time and increasing the chance of error.

🔴 Before — Original Uber Content (unstructured)

"You can cancel your ride at any time through the Uber app, but may be charged a cancellation fee if you cancel after you're matched with your driver. Cancellation fees pay drivers for the time and effort they spend getting to your location. For most economy ride options if rider canceled 2 or more minutes after requesting. For most Premium ride options if rider canceled 5 or more minutes after requesting. If your driver cancels a trip after they've been waiting at your pickup location for a specific amount of time, a cancellation fee will apply. These wait times vary depending on which Uber product you have requested. If you believe you were charged a cancellation fee in error, let us know."

Issues identified: No headings · Mixes rider and driver policies · No decision tree · No next steps · Not scannable · Difficult for agents to use quickly

The Solution: What I Built

I redesigned the article into a structured, modular HTML page organized around how an agent actually uses the content — starting with what happens next, moving into a decision tree for fee scenarios, and ending with clear step by step dispute instructions.

What I did:

  • Used Claude to help outline the content architecture and identify the key decision points a rider or agent would need to navigate
  • Hand-coded the redesigned article in HTML using inline styles following a consistent design pattern
  • Built a color-coded decision tree separating three fee scenarios — no charge, possible charge, and definite charge — so agents can find the right answer in seconds
  • Structured ride type wait times into a reference table for quick scanning
  • Applied metadata tags including content type, topic tags, audience, and last updated date to support search optimization and AI discoverability
  • Separated rider and driver cancellation policies into focused standalone sections to eliminate confusion
